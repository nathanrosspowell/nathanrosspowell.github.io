Keyboard Collection

Posted: 2015/08/03 22:27

My current hobby, customizing keyboards

Over the last year or so I have managed to gather four new keyboard, all of which I have modified in some manor. With a Korean custom made keyboard and several more sets of keycaps on the way it is time to document this problem.

Even among 'geeky' friend this collection/hobby seems to be very hard to understand. Keyboard are an everyday object to many of us, yet 99% of us are happy to have something totally bog-standard.

To me it is similar to having a bunch of sneakers. It's a collection but I also use all of what I own. They all do something slightly different but at the end of the day they all do the same job.

The Collection

Currently I have four keyboards, all have been heavily modified or are at least 'not stock'. This sounds like madness... and apart from them costing more than most people expect, it's a pretty tame hobby.

Why more than one?

So the easiest parallel I can draw to keyboards, with regards to having a collection, are shoes. People will have a wide variety of shoes these days - some are specific for running, some for basketball, some just to look good. The fashionable shoes don't have any tangible benefit over other shoes except they look better.

Keyboards are kind of the same. You can buy keyboards which excel in different areas - gaming, typing speed, ergonomic comfort, vintage, etc. On top of that you can go all out an modify the looks to your own preferences and tastes.

Unlike shoes, we are used to having one keyboard (normally attached to the laptop or free with the PC) and never having any problems with it. If it isn't broke, don't fix it, right? When we reach this line, it is something that is ubiquitous across all hobbies: once you get a taste for the sweeter side of life, there is no going back.

You walk down the street with your iPhone headphone on, life is going great! One of your friends wants to show you her new headphones that look all fancy. You try, they sounds great, but for $100... pffff. You go back to your iPhone headphones and now it feels like someone clogged your ears with cotton wool.

Favorite switch?

There are three 'classic' types of switches that most people use today. There is also a 'hybrid' switch that you can get on high end Japanese boards.

Linear

Cherry MX: Red, Black, Linear Grey
Gateron: Clear, Black, Yellow

These are switches that use the same force top to bottom, giving a linear force 'curve' when graphed out. Gamers are often drawn to this feeling as it is the best for rapid key mashing.

Tactile

Cherry MX: Brown, Clear, Grey
Gateron: Brown

Unlike linear switches, tactile switches have a 'dump' in their force curve just before the actuation point (the point where the key press registers). This allows you to feel the key press more. Browns have less of a bump than Clears and Greys, this makes then a good recommendation for a lot of people who might just be getting into keyboards.

Clicky

Cherry MX: Blue, Green, White
Gateron: Blue

These are like the linear switches in terms of force curve, but there is a part on the slider that produces a 'click'. This audio feedback is very important to a lot of people and it also gives a nice retro/vintage feel.

Rubberdome Capacitive

Topre: Variable weigh (different switches across the board), 45g, 55g

These are similar to the 'normal' keyboard construction you might be used to, except made to a far higher quality. There is a rubber cup molded for each switch with a spring on the inside to aid the tactile response.

My Choice

Most people never look back once they have a Topre board, stating it to be the best for tactile feedback. While I am definitely a tactile person, I still prefer tuned MX Clears (often referred to a ErgoClears). The large tactile bump with a slightly softer spring gives a very nice typing feel.

In some of my next builds I am going to use some other switch types to get a nice range available to play with. While switch testers are nice, it is often said that you can't get a real feel for the switch until you've used them on a full board. I have some Gateron Blacks (linear) and Cherry MX Blues (clicky) for my next builds.

Upcoming projects?

I have more soldering/wiring/programming projects coming up in the near future!

Leopold 210TP Rebuild

I bought a Leopold number pad to go with my Poker II (before I replaced it), but I never got used to the Cherry MX Red switches. This number pad also has a major flaw: the key codes it outputs are not the correct ones! If you hold shift and type 123 on your numpad you don't normally get !@# but on the Leopold you do.

I have removed the PCB completely and plan to rewire it by hand with a Leonardo Pro Micro ATmega32U4, using a mix of tuned Gateron switches. Id successful I would like to experiment with so function layers on the small layout, perhaps utilizing the top most four keys with LEDs to do some indication of which layer you are on. Maybe I will take this one into the office to go with the KUL and my incoming Hack'd By Geeks key set.

Ortholinear Keyboards Quark

While I do enjoy the 40% size of the Planck, I never really meshed with the Ortholinear layout. The Quark is the staggered twin of the Planck which I plan to wire up with a Teensy. I got a 'second' quality acrylic middle section for the case so I plan to do some LED lighting in the interior and maybe wire up the color to be indicative of the currently selected function layer.

I plan to set this up with Gateron Black (linear) switches Gateron PBT blank key caps that I have on order. Maybe I will attempt to paint the plate.

TX1800 by kin25

This will be my first (and last?!) Korean custom. The 1800 layout is fit into a full aluminum case with a PCB that needs A LOT of soldering done on it. It's going to be quite an interesting challenge to solder on the SMD (surface mount diodes) onto the PCB as they are the size of grain of rice.

To make the this 'perfect' custom I have bought a set of GMK HyperFuse key caps which have perfect 1800 layout support. I think I will set it up with Cherry MX Blue (clicky) switches to get a vintage feel on the board. Finally, I have a custom wrist rest on order from Noko.ca with some special artwork planned.
